$$

$$

## Regime inference: what the Markov-switching model gives us
From Chapter 3, the online Markov-switching filter gives the filtered posterior:
$$\alpha_{t|t}(j) = \Pr(S_t=j\mid y_{1:t}), \qquad j=1,\dots,K$$
The full filtered posterior vector is:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t} = (\alpha_{t|t}(1),\dots,\alpha_{t|t}(K))$$
This vector satisfies:
$$\alpha_{t|t}(j)\ge 0$$
and:
$$\sum_{j=1}^K \alpha_{t|t}(j)=1$$
It tells us how likely each latent regime is at time $t$, using observations available up to time $t$.
### Intuition
If:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t} = (0.08,0.92)$$
then the model strongly believes that the current observation belongs to regime 2.
But this is a statement about **current regime membership**, not directly about whether a changepoint occurred.
It answers:
> Which regime is currently likely?

It does **not** directly answer:
> Did a structural change just happen?

That distinction is the reason a separate detection layer is needed.
## Offline smoothed probabilities are not the detection object
In Chapter 3, you also introduced smoothed probabilities:
$$\gamma_t(j) = \Pr(S_t=j\mid y_{1:T})$$
These are useful for offline interpretation and EM estimation, because they use the whole sample.
But for online changepoint detection, they are not available at time $t$, because they use future observations:
$$y_{t+1},\dots,y_T$$
Therefore, Section 4.1 should already hint at the distinction:

|Quantity|Meaning|Online-valid?|
|---|---|---|
|$\alpha_{t|t}(j)$|filtered regime probability using $y_{1:t}$|
|$\gamma_t(j)$|smoothed regime probability using $y_{1:T}$|no, for live detection|

The online detector must be based on quantities available at time $t$.
That means the central posterior object is:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t}$$
not:
$$\gamma_t$$
The full causality argument belongs in Section 4.3. Here, it is enough to establish that online detection uses filtered inference, not smoothing.

## Why regime inference is not automatically changepoint detection
A high posterior probability for a regime is not the same thing as a changepoint.
Suppose:
\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t} = (0.02,0.98)
This tells us that regime 2 is very likely at time $t$.
But it does not tell us whether:
1. the process just switched into regime 2;
2. the process has been in regime 2 for a long time;
3. the model was already confident in regime 2 at time $t-1$.
To decide whether a changepoint occurred, we need to compare current information with previous information or evaluate how surprising the current observation was.
For example:
### Case A: high regime probability, no changepoint
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1} = (0.03,0.97)$$
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t} = (0.02,0.98)$$
The model is confident in regime 2 at both times.
There is no meaningful regime transition.
### Case B: high regime probability, possible changepoint
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1} = (0.95,0.05)$$
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t} = (0.02,0.98)$$
Now the model moved from high confidence in regime 1 to high confidence in regime 2.
This looks like a regime transition.
### Case C: no label flip, but posterior drift
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1} = (0.95,0.05)$$
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t} = (0.60,0.40)$$
The dominant regime is still regime 1, but the posterior has moved substantially.
A hard label detector may not fire, but a posterior-shift detector might.
### Case D: surprising observation, no regime shift
The posterior may remain:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t} \approx \boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1}$$
but the predictive density:
$$c_t = p(y_t\mid y_{1:t-1},\hat{\theta})$$
may be very small.
Then the observation was surprising under the model, even if it did not cause a regime switch.
This motivates the predictive surprise detector.
## What changepoint detection adds
A changepoint detector adds a decision layer on top of the Markov-switching filter.
It defines a scalar score:
$$s_t \in \mathbb{R}$$
and then converts that score into a binary alarm:
$$a_t \in \{0,1\}$$
A generic online detector can be written as:
$$s_t = g(y_{1:t})$$
This means the score at time $t$ is a function of the data observed so far.
In this thesis, the detector does not use the raw history directly. Instead, it uses quantities already computed by the online Markov-switching filter:
$$s_t = g( \boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t}, \boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1}, c_t )$$
where:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t} = (\alpha_{t|t}(1),\dots,\alpha_{t|t}(K))$$
is the current filtered posterior,
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1} = (\alpha_{t-1|t-1}(1),\dots,\alpha_{t-1|t-1}(K))$$
is the previous filtered posterior, and:
$$c_t = p(y_t\mid y_{1:t-1},\hat{\theta})$$
is the one-step predictive density.

The implementation also exposes the one-step-ahead **predicted posterior**
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t+1|t}(j) = \sum_i \hat{A}_{ij}\alpha_{t|t}(i)$$
as part of the detector input. It is causal (depends only on $y_{1:t}$) and is reserved for future detectors; none of the three detectors defined in Sections 4.4–4.6 consume it.

The alarm rule is:
$$a_t = \mathbf{1}[s_t\ge \tau]$$
where $\tau$ is a detector-specific threshold.
### Intuition
The Markov-switching filter answers:
> What does the model believe about regimes right now?

The detector answers:
> Is the current score large enough to call this a changepoint?

So the detector layer makes explicit what “change” means.
## Three possible meanings of “change” in this thesis
The word “changepoint” can mean different things depending on the detector score.
This chapter uses three detector semantics.
### Change as a dominant-regime switch
The first interpretation is:
> A changepoint occurs when the most probable regime changes.

Define:
$$\hat{S}_t = \arg\max_j \alpha_{t|t}(j)$$
Then a hard-switch score can be based on:
\hat{S}_t \ne \hat{S}_{t-1}
This gives a simple and interpretable detector.
It asks:
> Did the filtered posterior’s most likely regime label change?

This is the basis of Detector A.
### Change as posterior distribution movement
The second interpretation is:
> A changepoint occurs when the filtered posterior distribution changes substantially.

This avoids collapsing the posterior to a hard label.
For example, one can compare:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t}$$
and:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1}$$
A natural score is the total variation distance:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{TV}} = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{j=1}^K \left| \alpha_{t|t}(j) - \alpha_{t-1|t-1}(j) \right|$$
This score asks:
> How much did the entire posterior distribution move?

This is the basis of Detector B.
### Change as predictive surprise
The third interpretation is:
> A changepoint occurs when the new observation is unlikely under the model’s one-step prediction.

The predictive density is:
$$c_t = p(y_t\mid y_{1:t-1},\hat{\theta})$$
A natural surprise score is:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{surp}} = -\log c_t$$
Large surprise means that the observation was poorly predicted by the fitted model.
This is the basis of Detector C.
This detector does not directly ask whether the regime posterior changed.
It asks:
> Did the model fail to predict the new observation?

This can detect shocks or outliers even when the dominant regime does not change.
## Conventional changepoint methods as context
This section may briefly locate your approach relative to classical online changepoint detection methods.

### CUSUM
Page’s CUSUM is one of the classical sequential detection methods.
The basic idea is to accumulate evidence for a change using a running statistic. In the likelihood-ratio form, one often uses increments such as:
$$\ell_t = \log\frac{f_1(y_t)}{f_0(y_t)}$$
where:
- $f_0$ is the pre-change density;
- $f_1$ is the post-change density.

A cumulative statistic then grows when observations look more like the post-change model than the pre-change model.
The conceptual contrast is:

|CUSUM|This thesis|
|---|---|
|often compares fixed pre-change and post-change models|uses regimes learned by an offline MSM|
|accumulates evidence through a sequential statistic|computes scores from filtered posteriors or predictive density|
|classical sequential test|model-based detector layer on top of MSM inference|

Use CUSUM as context for sequential detection, citing Page:
```
\cite{Page1954}
```
### Bayesian Online Changepoint Detection
Bayesian Online Changepoint Detection, or BOCPD, maintains a posterior over the run length:
$$r_t = \text{number of observations since the last changepoint}$$
The Bayesian detector updates:
$$p(r_t\mid y_{1:t})$$
online.
The conceptual contrast is

|BOCPD|This thesis|
|---|---|
|explicitly models changepoints through run length|infers latent regimes and derives detector scores|
|maintains posterior over time since last changepoint|maintains posterior over current regime|
|changepoint is built into the state variable|changepoint is defined by detector semantics|

Your method is not BOCPD.
It does not maintain a run-length posterior.
Instead, it reuses the online Markov-switching filter:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t}$$
and the predictive density:
$$c_t$$
to produce changepoint scores.
Use Adams and MacKay for BOCPD context:
```
\cite{AdamsMacKay2007}
```

# 4.2 Offline-trained, online-filtered detector architecture
## What this section should do
This section should explain the **system architecture** of the proposed detector.
The key design is:
```
offline training → frozen fitted model → online filtering → detector score → alarm
```
The model is trained once on historical data. After training, the fitted parameters are frozen and used during online detection. During online operation, the model does **not** re-estimate parameters. It only updates:
1. the current filtering state;
2. the detector state;
3. the alarm-policy state.
The purpose of this section is to make the architecture clear before defining the individual detectors in Sections 4.4–4.6.
The central thesis point is:
> The proposed changepoint detector is not an online training algorithm. It is an online inference-and-detection algorithm based on an offline-trained Markov-switching model.

## Two-stage architecture
The architecture has two stages.
#### Offline training stage
In the offline stage, the model is trained on a historical training window:
$$y_{1:T_{\mathrm{train}}} = (y_1,\dots,y_{T_{\mathrm{train}}})$$
Using the EM procedure from Chapter 3, the model estimates:
$$\hat{\theta} = (\hat{\pi},\hat{A},\hat{\mu}_1,\dots,\hat{\mu}_K, $$$$\hat{\sigma}_1^2,\dots,\hat{\sigma}_K^2$$
Equivalently:
$$\hat{\theta} = \arg\max_{\theta} p(y_{1:T_{\mathrm{train}}}\mid\theta)$$
or, in log-likelihood form:
$$\hat{\theta} = \arg\max_{\theta} \ell_{\mathrm{train}}(\theta)$$
where:
$$\ell_{\mathrm{train}}(\theta) = \log p(y_{1:T_{\mathrm{train}}}\mid\theta)$$
You do **not** need to rederive EM here. That belongs to Chapter 3.
Here, just state that the offline stage produces a fitted Markov-switching model:
$$\hat{\theta}$$
### Intuition
The offline stage learns the statistical structure of the regimes from historical data.
It learns:
- how many regimes are represented by the chosen $K$;
- how persistent regimes are through $\hat{A}$;
- how observations behave in each regime through $\hat{\mu}_j$ and $\hat{\sigma}_j^2$.
After this stage, the model has a learned notion of what the regimes look like.
## Online detection stage
In the online stage, new observations arrive sequentially:
$$y_{T_{\mathrm{train}}+1}, y_{T_{\mathrm{train}}+2}, \dots$$
For notational simplicity, you can re-index the online period as:
$$y_1^{\mathrm{online}}, y_2^{\mathrm{online}}, \dots$$
or keep using global time $t$.
At each new time $t$, the detector receives one observation:
$$y_t$$
Using the frozen fitted parameters:
$$\hat{\theta}$$
the online filter computes:
$$\alpha_{t|t}(j) = \Pr(S_t=j\mid y_{1:t},\hat{\theta})$$
and the one-step predictive density:
$$c_t = p(y_t\mid y_{1:t-1},\hat{\theta})$$
These are then passed into a detector, which computes a score:
$s_t$
and then possibly an alarm:
$a_t \in \{0,1\}$
The online stage therefore has this structure:
```
new observation y_t
    ↓
online Hamilton filter with frozen θ̂
    ↓
filtered posterior α_{t|t} and predictive density c_t
    ↓
detector score s_t
    ↓
alarm decision a_t
```
### Intuition
The offline model says:
> These are the regimes I learned from history.

The online filter says:
> Given the new data so far, which learned regime is currently plausible?

The detector says:
> Did the filtering output or predictive fit change enough to call a changepoint?

## Frozen-parameter assumption
The defining architectural assumption is:
$$\hat{\theta} \quad \text{is fixed during online detection.}$$
That means:
$$\hat{\pi},\hat{A},\hat{\mu}_j,\hat{\sigma}_j^2$$
are not updated when new observations arrive.
Only the posterior state evolves:
$$\alpha_{t-1|t-1} \to \alpha_{t|t}$$
So the online phase is **not**:
$$\hat{\theta}_{t-1} \to \hat{\theta}_t$$
Instead, it is:
$$(\hat{\theta},\alpha_{t-1|t-1}) \to \alpha_{t|t}$$
### Why freeze the parameters?
Freezing the parameters gives a clean detector design.
It separates:

|Component|Role|
|---|---|
|offline training|learn regimes|
|online filtering|infer current regime under fixed model|
|detector layer|convert filter outputs into alarms|

This matters because if parameters were updated online, then a change could be absorbed by the model instead of detected.
For example, suppose the data distribution changes suddenly. If the model updates parameters immediately, it may adapt to the new distribution and reduce the detector’s ability to signal that a change occurred.
With frozen parameters, a structural mismatch appears as:
- posterior regime movement;
- a dominant-regime switch;
- low predictive density;
- increased surprise.
So freezing $\hat{\theta}$ makes changepoint signals more interpretable.
## What changes online?
Although $\hat{\theta}$ is fixed, the system is not static.
Three states can evolve online.
### Filtering state
The filtering state stores the current belief over regimes:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t} = (\alpha_{t|t}(1),\dots,\alpha_{t|t}(K))$$
When a new observation arrives, the filter updates:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1} \to \boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t}$$
This is the core online inference state.
### Detector state
Some detectors need previous information.
For example:
- hard switch detector stores the previous dominant regime;
- posterior transition detector stores the previous filtered posterior;
- surprise detector may store an EMA baseline.
So the detector may have its own state:
$$h_{t-1}^{\mathrm{det}} \to h_t^{\mathrm{det}}$$
### Alarm-policy state
The alarm policy may store:
- number of consecutive threshold crossings;
- cooldown counter;
- last alarm status.
So alarm logic also evolves:
$$h_{t-1}^{\mathrm{alarm}} \to h_t^{\mathrm{alarm}}$$
### Summary
During online detection:
$$\hat{\theta} \quad \text{is fixed,}$$
but:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t}, \quad h_t^{\mathrm{det}}, \quad h_t^{\mathrm{alarm}}$$
are updated sequentially.
## Online filtering with frozen parameters
At each online step, the Hamilton filter uses the fixed fitted parameters.
The prediction step is:
$$\alpha_{t|t-1}(j) = \sum_{i=1}^K \hat{A}_{ij}\alpha_{t-1|t-1}(i)$$
The predictive density is:
$$c_t = \sum_{j=1}^K f_j(y_t;\hat{\mu}_j,\hat{\sigma}_j^2) \alpha_{t|t-1}(j)$$
The update is:
$$\alpha_{t|t}(j) = \frac{ f_j(y_t;\hat{\mu}_j,\hat{\sigma}_j^2) \alpha_{t|t-1}(j) }{ c_t }$$
This is the same filter recursion as in Chapter 3, but used one observation at a time.
Do not rederive the filter here. The purpose is only to show how the fitted model is used online.
### Intuition
The online filter asks:
> Under the regimes learned offline, how should the posterior regime probabilities be updated after this new observation?

The detector then asks:
> Does this update indicate a changepoint according to the chosen score?

## 6. Detector input and output abstraction
The online filter produces quantities that become detector inputs.
A generic detector receives some subset of:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t}, \quad \boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1}, \quad c_t, \quad \log c_t$$
The detector computes:
$$s_t = g(\cdot)$$
and the alarm policy converts the score into:
$$a_t$$
### Mathematical abstraction
You can write the detector layer as:
$$s_t = g_D(I_t)$$
where $D$ is the detector type and $I_t$ is the detector input.
For example:
$$I_t = \left( \boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t}, \boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1}, c_t, \log c_t \right)$$
Then:
$$a_t = \mathcal{A}(s_t)$$
where $\mathcal{A}$ is the alarm policy.
The detailed forms of $g_D$ belong in Sections 4.4–4.6.
## The online step as a composed operation
The whole online step can be written as a composition.
Given:
- frozen parameters $\hat{\theta}$;
- previous filter state $\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1}$;
- detector state $h_{t-1}^{\mathrm{det}}$;
- alarm state $h_{t-1}^{\mathrm{alarm}}$;
- new observation $y_t$;
the system performs:
$$(\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t}, c_t) = \mathrm{FilterStep} (y_t,\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1};\hat{\theta})$$
Then:
$$(s_t,h_t^{\mathrm{det}}) = \mathrm{DetectorStep} (\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t},c_t,h_{t-1}^{\mathrm{det}})$$
Then:
$$(a_t,h_t^{\mathrm{alarm}}) = \mathrm{AlarmPolicy} (s_t,h_{t-1}^{\mathrm{alarm}})$$
So the full update is:
$$(y_t,\hat{\theta}, \boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1}, h_{t-1}^{\mathrm{det}}, h_{t-1}^{\mathrm{alarm}}) \mapsto (\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t},s_t,a_t, h_t^{\mathrm{det}}, h_t^{\mathrm{alarm}})$$
### Intuition
This makes the architecture modular.
The filter is responsible for probabilistic inference.
The detector is responsible for defining a changepoint score.
The alarm policy is responsible for deciding when the score becomes an event.

This is the **causality constraint**.
In this chapter, the detector is online only if every score and alarm can be computed when the new observation $y_t$ arrives.

## Definition of a causal detector
A detector produces a score:
$$s_t$$
and usually an alarm:
$$a_t \in \{0,1\}$$
A detector is **causal** if there exists some function $g_t$ such that:
$$s_t = g_t(y_{1:t})$$
and no future observation appears in the input.
Equivalently:
$$s_t$$
must be measurable with respect to the information set:
$$\mathcal{F}_t = \sigma(y_1,\dots,y_t)$$
You do not need to overdevelop measure-theoretic notation in the thesis, but this notation is useful because it states the idea precisely:
> At time $t$, the detector may only use information contained in $\mathcal{F}_t$.

The alarm is then also causal if:
$$a_t = h_t(s_1,\dots,s_t)$$
or, more generally, if it depends only on current and past scores.
For a simple threshold rule:
$$a_t = \mathbf{1}[s_t \ge \tau]$$
causality follows immediately if $s_t$ is causal.
For persistence and cooldown, the alarm may depend on earlier threshold crossings, but still only on past and present information. So it remains causal.
## Why this matters
The point of online changepoint detection is to issue alarms as data arrive.
If a detector uses future data, then it is not a live detector. It is a retrospective labeling method.
For example, if an alarm at time $t$ uses:
$$y_{t+5}$$
then the alarm could not actually have been issued at time $t$.
This would create look-ahead bias.
In financial applications, this is especially important:
- trading decisions cannot use future prices;
- monitoring systems cannot use observations that have not arrived;
- evaluation of detection delay becomes invalid if future data leak into the detector.
So this section should clearly separate:

| Method              | Information used       | Valid for live detection?      |
| ------------------- | ---------------------- | ------------------------------ |
| filtering           | $y_{1:t}$              | yes                            |
| smoothing           | $y_{1:T}$              | no                             |
| offline diagnostics | full historical sample | no, not as live detector input |
## Filtering is causal
The filtered posterior is:
$$\alpha_{t|t}(j) = \Pr(S_t=j\mid y_{1:t})$$
This conditions only on:
$$y_1,\dots,y_t$$
Therefore:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t} = (\alpha_{t|t}(1),\dots,\alpha_{t|t}(K))$$
is causal.
It can be computed when observation $y_t$ arrives.
In the online detector, the filtered posterior is the main regime-inference input.
### Intuition
At time $t$, the filter says:
> Given everything seen so far, what regime is currently plausible?

It does not know the future.
That makes it valid for online detection.
## Smoothing is non-causal
The smoothed posterior is:
$$\gamma_t(j) = \Pr(S_t=j\mid y_{1:T})$$
For $t<T$, this conditions on:
$$y_{t+1},\dots,y_T$$
Therefore, smoothing uses future observations relative to time $t$.
So it is not valid as a live detector input.
### Intuition
Smoothing answers:
> After seeing the entire sequence, what regime probably occurred at time $t$?

That is useful for retrospective analysis, visualization, and EM training.
But it cannot be used to issue an alarm at time $t$.
### Important thesis wording
Use this distinction carefully:
> Smoothed probabilities are used in offline estimation and retrospective interpretation, but online detection uses only filtered probabilities.

Avoid implying that smoothed probabilities are “better” for detection. They may be more accurate retrospectively, but they are not available in real time.
## Causal online filtering recursion
The online detector uses the fitted parameters:
$$\hat{\theta} = (\hat{\pi},\hat{A},\hat{\mu},\hat{\sigma}^2)$$
from offline training.
At each time $t$, the online filter takes:
- previous filtered posterior:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1}$$
- new observation:
$$y_t$$
- frozen model parameters:
$$\hat{\theta}$$
and computes:
- predicted posterior:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t-1}$$
- predictive density:
$$c_t$$
- updated filtered posterior:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t}$$
The recursion is:
### Prediction
$$\alpha_{t|t-1}(j) = \sum_{i=1}^K \hat{A}_{ij} \alpha_{t-1|t-1}(i)$$
### Predictive density
$$c_t = \sum_{j=1}^K f_j(y_t;\hat{\mu}_j,\hat{\sigma}_j^2) \alpha_{t|t-1}(j)$$
### Update
$$\alpha_{t|t}(j) = \frac{ f_j(y_t;\hat{\mu}_j,\hat{\sigma}_j^2) \alpha_{t|t-1}(j) }{ c_t }$$
This is the same filtering recursion introduced earlier, but here the emphasis is different:
> Every quantity on the right-hand side is available at time $t$.

No future observations are used.
## Why each detector input is causal
The detector receives quantities such as:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t}, \quad \boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1}, \quad c_t, \quad \log c_t$$
Each one is causal.
### Current filtered posterior
\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t}
depends only on:
$$y_{1:t}$$
So it is causal.
### Previous filtered posterior
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1}$$
depends only on:
$$y_{1:t-1}$$
Since:
$$y_{1:t-1}\subset y_{1:t}$$
it is also available at time $t$.
### Predictive density
$$c_t = p(y_t\mid y_{1:t-1},\hat{\theta})$$
This uses the current observation $y_t$ and the past filtering state.
It does not use future observations.
So it is causal.
### Log predictive density
$$\log c_t$$
is just a transformation of $c_t$, so it is also causal.
## Causality of the three detector families
This section should not fully define the detectors yet. That belongs in Sections 4.4–4.6.
But it should state why each detector type can be causal.
### Hard switch detector
The hard switch detector compares dominant regimes:
$$\hat{S}_t = \arg\max_j \alpha_{t|t}(j)$$
and:
$$\hat{S}_{t-1} = \arg\max_j \alpha_{t-1|t-1}(j)$$
Both posteriors are available at time $t$.
So the score is causal.
### Posterior transition detector
A posterior transition score compares:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t}$$
and:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1}$$
Both are available at time $t$.
So it is causal.
### Predictive surprise detector
Predictive surprise uses:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{surp}} = -\log c_t$$
Since $c_t$ is computed from $y_t$ and $y_{1:t-1}$, this is causal.
If an EMA baseline is used, the causal form is:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{adj}} = s_t^{\mathrm{surp}} - b_{t-1}$$
where:
$$b_{t-1}$$
was computed from previous scores only.
The update:
$$b_t = \alpha_{\mathrm{EMA}}s_t^{\mathrm{surp}} + (1-\alpha_{\mathrm{EMA}})b_{t-1}$$
happens after scoring $y_t$.
So the score uses $b_{t-1}$, not $b_t$.
This prevents the baseline from absorbing the current observation before the score is computed.
## Causality of alarm policy
The alarm policy may use thresholding, persistence, and cooldown.
A raw threshold alarm is:
$$a_t^{\mathrm{raw}} = \mathbf{1}[s_t\ge \tau]$$
This is causal if $s_t$ is causal.
A persistence rule may require $N_{\mathrm{req}}$ consecutive crossings:
$$a_t = 1$$
only if:
$$s_{t-N_{\mathrm{req}}+1},\dots,s_t$$
all crossed the threshold.
This is still causal because it uses only current and past scores.
A cooldown rule suppresses alarms for $C$ steps after an alarm.
This is also causal because it only depends on the time since the last alarm.
So the full alarm policy remains causal.
The key point:
> Persistence and cooldown may delay or suppress alarms, but they do not introduce future information.

Some operations are valid during training or retrospective evaluation but invalid during live detection.

Offline training and live detection have different information sets.
Training can use the whole historical training window.
Live detection must behave as if the future does not exist.
This distinction should be explicit in the thesis because otherwise the evaluation of an “online” algorithm becomes invalid.

# 4.4 Detector A: hard switch detector
## What this section should do
This section should define the **simplest changepoint detector** built on top of the online Markov-switching filter.
The idea is:
> A changepoint is detected when the most probable filtered regime changes from one time step to the next.

The online filter gives a posterior distribution over regimes:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t} = (\alpha_{t|t}(1),\dots,\alpha_{t|t}(K))$$
The hard switch detector collapses this soft posterior distribution into a single dominant regime label:
$$\hat{S}_t = \arg\max_{j \in \{1,\dots,K\}} \alpha_{t|t}(j)$$
Then it checks whether this dominant label changed:
$$\hat{S}_t \ne \hat{S}_{t-1}$$
If the dominant label changed, the detector produces a switch signal.

- how the hard switch score is computed;

- what the detector means intuitively;
- when it works well;
- when it can fail;

$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t}$$
Do **not** discuss smoothing.
The hard switch detector is online and uses filtered probabilities only.

## Input to the detector
The hard switch detector uses the filtered posterior at time $t$:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t} = (\alpha_{t|t}(1),\dots,\alpha_{t|t}(K))$$
It also needs information from the previous time step, specifically the previous dominant regime:
$$\hat{S}_{t-1}$$
So conceptually, the detector uses:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t} \quad \text{and} \quad \hat{S}_{t-1}$$
It does **not** use
- smoothed probabilities;
- future observations;
- pairwise transition probabilities;
- EM updates;
- full historical re-estimation.
This keeps the detector causal and lightweight.
## Dominant filtered regime
The filtered posterior tells us the probability of each regime at time $t$:
$$\alpha_{t|t}(j) = \Pr(S_t=j \mid y_{1:t})$$
The hard switch detector converts this posterior vector into a single regime label by taking the maximum-probability regime:
$$\hat{S}_t = \arg\max_j \alpha_{t|t}(j)$$
This is the **dominant filtered regime**.
### Intuition
If:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t} = (0.10,0.85,0.05)$$
then:
$$\hat{S}_t = 2$$
The model assigns the largest posterior probability to regime 2.
So, for the hard switch detector, time $t$ is summarized as:
> dominant regime = 2.

The detector intentionally discards the rest of the posterior distribution.
That is why it is called “hard”: it converts soft probabilities into a hard label.
## Basic hard switch score
The simplest hard switch score is:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{HS}} = \mathbf{1}[\hat{S}_t \ne \hat{S}_{t-1}]$$
This score takes values in:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{HS}} \in \{0,1\}$$
It is equal to one if the dominant filtered regime changed.
It is equal to zero if the dominant filtered regime stayed the same.
### Cases
If:
$$\hat{S}_{t-1}=1$$
and:
$$\hat{S}_t=2$$
then:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{HS}}=1$$
If:
$$\hat{S}_{t-1}=2$$
and:
$$\hat{S}_t=2$$
then:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{HS}}=0$$
### Intuition
The detector asks only:
> Did the most probable regime label change?

It does not ask how much probability moved.
It does not ask whether the new observation was surprising.
It does not ask whether the transition probability under $\hat{A}$ was large or small.
It only detects a change in the posterior argmax.
## Confidence gate
A raw argmax switch can be unstable when the posterior is uncertain.
For example:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1} = (0.51,0.49)$$
and:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t} = (0.49,0.51)$$
The dominant label changes from regime 1 to regime 2.
So the raw hard switch detector gives:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{HS}}=1$$
But the model is barely more confident in regime 2 than regime 1.
This may be a weak signal.
To avoid alarms from uncertain argmax flips, use a confidence threshold:
$$\tau_{\mathrm{conf}} \in (0,1]$$
The confidence level at time $t$ is:
$$q_t = \max_j \alpha_{t|t}(j)$$
Then the confidence-gated hard switch score is:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{HS}} = \mathbf{1}[\hat{S}_t \ne \hat{S}_{t-1}] \cdot \mathbf{1} \left[ \max_j \alpha_{t|t}(j) \ge \tau_{\mathrm{conf}} \right]$$
Equivalently:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{HS}}=1$$
only if both conditions hold:
1. the dominant regime changed;
2. the current dominant regime is sufficiently probable.
## Intuition behind the confidence gate
The confidence gate prevents the detector from reacting to weak posterior fluctuations.
### Unconfident switch
Suppose:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1} = (0.51,0.49)$$
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t} = (0.49,0.51)$$
The argmax changes.
But:
$$max_j \alpha_{t|t}(j)=0.51$$
If:
$$\tau_{\mathrm{conf}}=0.60$$
then:
$$0.51 < 0.60$$
So:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{HS}}=0$$
No hard switch alarm is produced.
### Confident switch
Now suppose:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1} = (0.90,0.10)$$
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t} = (0.15,0.85)$$
The argmax changes from regime 1 to regime 2.
Also:
$$\max_j \alpha_{t|t}(j)=0.85$$
If:
$$\tau_{\mathrm{conf}}=0.60$$
then:
$$0.85 \ge 0.60$$
So:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{HS}}=1$$
This is a much stronger switch signal.
## Score domain and alarm rule
The hard switch score is binary:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{HS}}\in\{0,1\}$$
Because the score is already $\{0,1\}$-valued, the implementation does not introduce a separate numeric threshold for it: the hard-switch score *is itself the threshold-crossing indicator* passed to the persistence policy. Equivalently, the detector evaluates
$$\text{threshold\_crossed}_t = \big(\hat{S}_t \ne \hat{S}_{t-1}\big) \wedge \big(\max_j\alpha_{t|t}(j) \ge \tau_{\mathrm{conf}}\big)$$
and feeds this Boolean directly into the persistence/cooldown stabiliser of Section 4.7.
The general alarm policy from Section 4.7 still applies on top of this indicator:
- persistence (require $N_{\mathrm{req}}$ consecutive switches);
- cooldown (suppress repeated alarms after one fires).
No extra threshold $\tau$ on $s_t^{\mathrm{HS}}$ is needed or used.
## First-step warmup
The hard switch detector compares:
$$\hat{S}_t$$
with:
$$\hat{S}_{t-1}$$
At the first online observation, there is no previous dominant regime.
Therefore, the first step cannot produce a valid switch comparison.
So the detector must initialize:
$$\hat{S}_1$$
and return:
$$\text{ready} = \text{false}$$
for the first step.
From the second step onward, the detector can compare:
$$\hat{S}_t \quad \text{against} \quad \hat{S}_{t-1}$$
### Intuition
A switch is a relation between two time points.
At the first point, there is no previous point to compare with.
So the first observation is used to initialize the detector state.
## What the detector actually detects
The hard switch detector detects:
$$\arg\max_j \alpha_{t|t}(j) \ne \arg\max_j \alpha_{t-1|t-1}(j)$$
So its semantic meaning is:
> The most probable filtered regime changed.

It does **not** directly detect every possible kind of changepoint.
It does not detect:
- small posterior drift if the argmax does not change;
- high predictive surprise if the posterior label remains the same;
- gradual uncertainty build-up unless it eventually changes the argmax.
Therefore, a hard switch alarm should be interpreted specifically as:
> an argmax regime transition under the online filtered posterior.

Not as:
> proof of a true structural changepoint.
## Example: sharp regime switch
Suppose $K=2$.
At time $t-1$:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1} = (0.92,0.08)$$
Then:
$$\hat{S}_{t-1}=1$$
At time $t$:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t} = (0.20,0.80)$$
Then:
$$\hat{S}_t=2$$
The dominant regime changed.
If:
$$\tau_{\mathrm{conf}}=0.60$$
then:
$$\max_j \alpha_{t|t}(j)=0.80\ge 0.60$$
So:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{HS}}=1$$
The detector fires.
### Interpretation
This is the setting where the hard switch detector works best:
- the posterior moved sharply;
- the new dominant regime is clear;
- the argmax changed decisively.
## Example: gradual transition missed by hard switch
Suppose:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-2|t-2} = (0.90,0.10)$$
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1} = (0.70,0.30)$$
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t} = (0.55,0.45)$$
The posterior is clearly drifting from regime 1 toward regime 2.
But the dominant regime remains regime 1:
$$\hat{S}_{t-2} = \hat{S}_{t-1} = \hat{S}_t = 1$$
So:
$$s_{t-1}^{\mathrm{HS}}=0$$
and:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{HS}}=0$$
### Interpretation
The hard switch detector misses this gradual change because the argmax has not flipped.
This is why Section 4.5 introduces posterior transition detectors, which measure posterior movement rather than only dominant-label changes.
## Example: unstable boundary oscillation
Suppose:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-2|t-2} = (0.51,0.49)$$
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1} = (0.49,0.51)$$
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t} = (0.52,0.48)$$
The dominant label flips repeatedly:
$$1 \to 2 \to 1$$
A hard switch detector without a confidence gate might fire repeatedly.
But the posterior is uncertain throughout.
A confidence gate such as:
$$\tau_{\mathrm{conf}}=0.60$$
suppresses these alarms because:
$$\max_j \alpha_{t|t}(j)<0.60$$
### Interpretation
The confidence threshold is especially useful when posterior probabilities hover around the decision boundary.
## Strengths of the hard switch detector
The hard switch detector has three main strengths.
### Interpretability
The score has a simple meaning:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{HS}}=1$$
means:
> the dominant filtered regime changed.

This is easy to explain and easy to visualize.
### Low computational cost
The detector only needs:
- an argmax over $K$ regimes;
- a comparison with the previous dominant regime;
- optionally, a maximum posterior probability check.
So its cost per time step is:
$$O(K)$$
This is negligible compared to the filtering step, which is usually:
$$O(K^2)$$
### Natural fit for sharp regime shifts
If the fitted Markov-switching model produces clear, abrupt posterior transitions, the hard switch detector captures them directly.
## Limitations of the hard switch detector
The hard switch detector is simple, but it loses information.
### It discards posterior uncertainty
The posterior vector:
$$(0.51,0.49)$$
and:
$$(0.99,0.01)$$
both give dominant regime 1.
But they represent very different levels of certainty.
The confidence gate partly addresses this, but the hard label still discards most of the posterior information.
### It can miss gradual changes
If the posterior moves substantially but the argmax does not change, the detector does not fire.
Example:
$$(0.95,0.05) \to (0.70,0.30)$$
The posterior moved, but the dominant regime is still 1.
### It can be unstable near the boundary
If posterior probabilities oscillate around equality, the argmax may flip repeatedly.
The confidence threshold and persistence policy can reduce this issue.
### It detects label switches, not necessarily structural breaks
A label switch is a model-internal event.
It is evidence of a regime transition according to the fitted model, but it is not direct proof of an externally defined changepoint.
This distinction should be explicit in the thesis.
## Relationship to posterior transition and surprise detectors
This section should briefly position the hard switch detector relative to later detectors.
The hard switch detector uses:
$$\arg\max_j \alpha_{t|t}(j)$$
The posterior transition detector uses more of the posterior distribution, for example:
$$\frac{1}{2} \sum_j |\alpha_{t|t}(j)-\alpha_{t-1|t-1}(j)|$$
The surprise detector uses:
$$-\log c_t$$
So the hard switch detector is the most discrete and interpretable detector, but also the least sensitive to gradual changes or predictive anomalies.

$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t} = (\alpha_{t|t}(1),\dots,\alpha_{t|t}(K))$$
Instead of asking only:

this detector asks:
> Did the posterior distribution over regimes move substantially?

This is useful because a meaningful transition may begin before the dominant regime changes.

## Input to the detector
The posterior transition detector uses two consecutive filtered posterior vectors:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1}$$
and:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t}$$
These are both causal quantities.
The previous posterior:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1}$$
was computed at the previous step.
The current posterior:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t}$$
is computed after observing $y_t$.
So the detector uses:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1}, \quad \boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t}$$
and produces a score:
$$s_t$$
It does not use:
- smoothed probabilities;
- future observations;
- pairwise transition probabilities;
- EM updates.
## Why this detector is needed
The hard switch detector can miss gradual changes.
For example, suppose $K=2$ and the posterior sequence is:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-2|t-2} = (0.95,0.05)$$
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1} = (0.75,0.25)$$
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t} = (0.58,0.42)$$
The dominant regime is still regime 1 at every step:
$$\hat{S}_{t-2} = \hat{S}_{t-1} = \hat{S}_t = 1$$
So the hard switch detector does not fire.
But the posterior distribution clearly moved toward regime 2.
The posterior transition detector is designed to detect exactly this kind of movement.
## Detector B1: leave-previous score
The first posterior transition score is the **leave-previous score**.
Define the previously dominant regime:
$$r_{t-1} = \arg\max_j \alpha_{t-1|t-1}(j)$$
This is the regime that was most probable at the previous time step.
The leave-previous score is:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{LP}} = 1-\alpha_{t|t}(r_{t-1})$$
This score measures how much posterior mass has left the previously dominant regime.
## Intuition behind leave-previous
Suppose the previous dominant regime was:
$$r_{t-1}=1$$
Then the score is:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{LP}} = 1-\alpha_{t|t}(1)$$
If the current posterior still strongly supports regime 1, for example:
$$\alpha_{t|t}(1)=0.95$$
then:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{LP}} = 1-0.95 = 0.05$$
The score is small.
But if the current posterior assigns much less mass to regime 1, for example:
$$\alpha_{t|t}(1)=0.35$$
then:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{LP}} = 1-0.35 = 0.65$$
The score is large.
### Interpretation
The detector asks:
> How much has the model stopped believing in the regime it believed in previously?

This is more sensitive than a hard label switch.

A hard switch waits until the argmax changes.
The leave-previous score increases as soon as mass starts leaving the previous dominant regime.
## Range of the leave-previous score
Because:
$$0 \le \alpha_{t|t}(r_{t-1}) \le 1$$
we have:
$$0 \le 1-\alpha_{t|t}(r_{t-1}) \le 1$$
Therefore:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{LP}} \in [0,1]$$
### Meaning of extremes
If:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{LP}}=0$$
then:
$$\alpha_{t|t}(r_{t-1})=1$$
The model assigns all posterior mass to the previously dominant regime.
If:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{LP}}=1$$
then:
$$\alpha_{t|t}(r_{t-1})=0$$
The model assigns no posterior mass to the previously dominant regime.
In practice, the score usually lies between these extremes.
## Example: leave-previous detector
Suppose:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1} = (0.80,0.20)$$
Then:
$$r_{t-1}=1$$
Now suppose:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t} = (0.55,0.45)$$
The hard switch detector does not fire, because regime 1 is still dominant.
But the leave-previous score is:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{LP}} = 1-\alpha_{t|t}(1) = 1-0.55 = 0.45.$$
This shows a substantial weakening of belief in the previous dominant regime.
If the threshold is:
$$\tau=0.40$$
then:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{LP}} \ge \tau$$
so this detector can produce a signal before the dominant label changes.
## Limitation of leave-previous
The leave-previous score depends on the previous dominant regime:
$$r_{t-1}$$
This makes it intuitive, but also asymmetric.
It asks only:
> How much mass left the previously dominant regime?

It does not directly measure the full distance between two posterior distributions.
For example, if probability mass moves among non-dominant regimes, the leave-previous score may not react much.
Suppose:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1} = (0.70,0.20,0.10)$$
so:
$$r_{t-1}=1$$
Now suppose:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t} = (0.70,0.05,0.25)$$
Then:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{LP}} = 1-\alpha_{t|t}(1) = 0.30$$
But the score is unchanged relative to the previous dominant mass, even though probability moved substantially from regime 2 to regime 3.
This motivates a second score using the full posterior-vector difference.
## Detector B2: total variation score
The second posterior transition score is based on the distance between consecutive posterior distributions.
Define:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{TV}} = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{j=1}^K \left| \alpha_{t|t}(j) - \alpha_{t-1|t-1}(j) \right|$$
This is the **total variation distance** between the two posterior distributions:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t}$$
and:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1}$$
It measures how much the whole posterior vector changed.
## Intuition behind total variation
The total variation score asks:
> How much posterior probability mass must be moved to transform the previous posterior distribution into the current one?

If the two posterior distributions are identical:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t} = \boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1}$$
then:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{TV}}=0$$
If they are completely different, for example:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1} = (1,0)$$
and:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t} = (0,1)$$
then:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{TV}} = \frac{1}{2}(|0-1|+|1-0|) = 1$$
So:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{TV}}\in[0,1]$$
## Why the factor $\frac{1}{2}$ appears
The sum:
$$∣\sum_{j=1}^K \left| \alpha_{t|t}(j) - \alpha_{t-1|t-1}(j) \right|j=1$$
is the $L^1$ distance between the two posterior vectors.
For probability distributions, the maximum $L^1$ distance is $2$.
Example:
$$(1,0) \quad \text{and} \quad (0,1)$$
have:
$$|1-0|+|0-1|=2$$
Multiplying by:
$$\frac{1}{2}$$
normalizes the score to the interval:
$$[0,1]$$
Thus:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{TV}} = \frac{1}{2}\|\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t}-\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1}\|_1$$
This makes the score easier to threshold and compare.
## Example: total variation detector
Suppose:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1} = (0.80,0.20)$$
and:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t} = (0.55,0.45)$$
Then:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{TV}} = \frac{1}{2} \left( |0.55-0.80| + |0.45-0.20| \right)$$
So:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{TV}} = \frac{1}{2} (0.25+0.25) = 0.25$$
This means that 25% of posterior mass shifted between regimes.
In a two-state model, this has a simple interpretation: the total variation distance equals the absolute change in one regime’s probability.
Indeed:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{TV}} = |\alpha_{t|t}(1)-\alpha_{t-1|t-1}(1)|$$
when $K=2$.
## Leave-previous versus total variation
The two scores capture related but different ideas.

|Score|Formula|Meaning|
|---|---|---|
|Leave-previous|$1-\alpha_{t|t}(r_{t-1})$|
|Total variation|$\frac12\sum_j|\alpha_{t|

### Leave-previous is more directional
It focuses on the previously dominant regime.
It is useful when the detector is specifically interested in leaving the current regime.
### Total variation is more symmetric
It treats both posterior distributions as full probability vectors.
It does not privilege the previous dominant regime.
It detects any large posterior redistribution.
## Important difference from hard switch detector
The hard switch detector uses:
$$\hat{S}_t = \arg\max_j \alpha_{t|t}(j)$$
The posterior transition detector uses posterior probabilities directly.
Therefore, it preserves more information.
### Example
Suppose:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1} = (0.95,0.05)$$
and:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t} = (0.60,0.40)$$
The dominant regime remains 1.
Hard switch:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{HS}}=0$$
Leave-previous:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{LP}} = 1-0.60 = 0.40$$
Total variation:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{TV}} = \frac12(|0.60-0.95|+|0.40-0.05|) = 0.35$$
So the posterior transition detector can signal meaningful movement before a hard switch occurs.
## Thresholding the score
Both posterior transition scores lie in:
$$[0,1]$$
A basic alarm rule is:
$$a_t = \mathbf{1}[s_t \ge \tau]$$
where:
$$\tau\in[0,1]$$
For example:
- small $\tau$ makes the detector more sensitive;
- large $\tau$ makes the detector more conservative.
In practice, this score may also pass through the common alarm policy with:
- persistence;
- cooldown.
Those details belong to Section 4.7.
Here it is enough to state that posterior transition detectors produce continuous scores that are later thresholded.
## First-step warmup
The posterior transition detector requires:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1}$$
and:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t}$$
At the first online observation, there is no previous filtered posterior from the online stream.
Therefore, the first step is used for initialization.
At that step:
- store the current posterior;
- return no valid transition score or mark `ready = false`.
From the second step onward, the detector can compute posterior movement.
### Intuition
A transition score compares two consecutive posterior distributions.
At the first observation, only one posterior distribution exists.
So no comparison is possible yet.
## What the posterior transition detector detects
This detector detects:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1} \to \boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t}$$
movement.
Its semantic meaning is:
> The model’s filtered belief over regimes changed substantially.

It does not necessarily mean:
- the dominant regime changed;
- the observation was predictively surprising;
- a true external structural break occurred.
It means the posterior regime distribution moved according to the chosen score.
This interpretation should be explicit.
## Strengths of posterior transition detectors
### They preserve more information than hard labels
They use posterior probabilities directly, not only the argmax.
This makes them more sensitive to uncertainty changes and gradual regime shifts.
### They can detect pre-switch drift
They can fire before the dominant regime changes.
This is useful when the model gradually shifts probability mass from one regime to another.
### Scores are normalized
Both scores lie in:
$$[0,1]$$
This makes thresholding more interpretable than for unbounded scores such as raw surprise.
### Total variation is label-symmetric
The total variation score does not depend on choosing a previous dominant regime.
It measures full posterior movement.
## Limitations of posterior transition detectors
### They may fire on uncertainty changes
A posterior distribution can move because the model becomes less certain, not necessarily because a regime transition occurred.
Example:
$$(0.95,0.05) \to (0.55,0.45)$$
This is a large posterior movement, but the dominant regime may remain the same.
Whether this is a changepoint depends on the detector semantics.
### They do not directly measure predictive misfit
The posterior can remain stable even if:
$$c_t$$
is low.
That means the new observation was surprising, but the posterior still assigned it to the same regime.
This is why the predictive surprise detector is introduced separately.
### Leave-previous depends on a hard label
Although leave-previous uses posterior mass, it still depends on:
$$r_{t-1} = \arg\max_j \alpha_{t-1|t-1}(j)$$
So it inherits some sensitivity to the previous dominant regime.
### Total variation does not say where the mass moved
The score measures how much movement occurred, but not which transition occurred.
For interpretability, one may inspect the posterior vectors themselves.

The posterior transition detector compares consecutive filtered posteriors:

# 4.6 Detector C: predictive surprise detector
## What this section should do
This section should define the third detector: the **predictive surprise detector**.
The previous two detectors were based on posterior regime probabilities:
- hard switch detector: did the dominant regime change?
- posterior transition detector: did the filtered posterior distribution move?
The predictive surprise detector asks a different question:
> Was the new observation unlikely under the model’s one-step-ahead predictive distribution?

So this detector is not primarily about whether the posterior regime label changed. It is about whether the fitted Markov-switching model predicted the next observation well.
The central quantity is the one-step-ahead predictive density:
$$c_t = p(y_t\mid y_{1:t-1},\hat{\theta})$$
The raw surprise score is:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{surp}} = -\log c_t$$
A large value means:
> The observation $y_t$ was unexpected under the offline-trained Markov-switching model.

## Input to the detector

The predictive surprise detector uses the one-step-ahead predictive density produced by the online filter:
$$c_t = p(y_t\mid y_{1:t-1},\hat{\theta})$$
or equivalently its logarithm:
$$\log c_t$$
It does **not** need:
- the dominant regime label;
- the previous dominant regime;
- the full posterior difference;
- smoothed probabilities;
- pairwise transition probabilities;
- future observations.
It may optionally use an adaptive baseline:
$$b_{t-1}$$
computed from previous surprise scores.
So the core detector input is:
log⁡ct\log c_tlogct​
and the optional state is:
$$b_{t-1}$$
## Predictive density
The predictive density is:
$$c_t = p(y_t\mid y_{1:t-1},\hat{\theta})$$
In the Markov-switching model, the current regime is unknown, so the predictive density marginalizes over possible regimes:
$$c_t = \sum_{j=1}^K f_j(y_t;\hat{\mu}_j,\hat{\sigma}_j^2) \alpha_{t|t-1}(j)$$
Here:

|Term|Meaning|
|---|---|
|$f_j(y_t;\hat{\mu}_j,\hat{\sigma}_j^2)$|likelihood of $y_t$ under regime $j$|
|$\alpha_{t|t-1}(j)$|
|$c_t$|total one-step predictive density of $y_t$|

The predictive density is therefore a weighted average of regime-specific densities, where the weights are the predicted regime probabilities.
### Intuition
Before observing $y_t$, the model has a belief about the next regime:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t-1}$$
Then $y_t$ arrives.
The model asks:
> Under the regimes I expected before seeing $y_t$, how plausible is this new value?

If $y_t$ is plausible under likely regimes, then $c_t$ is large.
If $y_t$ is unlikely under all likely regimes, then $c_t$ is small.
## Raw surprise score
The raw predictive surprise score is:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{surp}} = -\log c_t$$
Since:
$$c_t = p(y_t\mid y_{1:t-1},\hat{\theta})$$
we can write:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{surp}} = -\log p(y_t\mid y_{1:t-1},\hat{\theta})$$
This is the **negative log predictive density**.
### Intuition
If the model assigns high predictive density to $y_t$, then:
$$c_t​$$
is large and:
$$-\log c_t$$
is small.
So the observation was not surprising.
If the model assigns low predictive density to $y_t$, then:
$$c_t$$
is smalland:
$$-\log c_t$$
is large.
So the observation was surprising.
## Relationship to log-likelihood
From the filtering likelihood decomposition:
$$\ell(\hat{\theta}) = \sum_{t=1}^T \log c_t$$
Therefore:
$$-\log c_t$$
is th neative contribution of observation $y_t$ to the online log-likelihood.
So the surprise score is not arbitrary. It is exactly the per-time-step negative predictive log-likelihood:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{surp}} = -\log c_t$$
### Intuition
The model’s total log-likelihood rewards observations that were predicted well.
The surprise detector looks at the opposite quantity at each time step:
> How much did this observation hurt the predictive likelihood?

A large surprise score means that the current observation is a poor fit under the model’s one-step predictive distribution.
## Example
Suppose the predictive density is:
$$c_t=0.50$$
Then:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{surp}} = -\log(0.50) \approx 0.693$$
This is relatively small.
Now suppose:
$$c_t=0.001$$
Then:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{surp}} = -\log(0.001) \approx 6.908$$
This is much larger.
### Interpretation
The second observation is far more surprising under the model.
So if the surprise threshold is, for example:
$$\tau=5$$
then:
$$6.908 \ge 5$$
would trigger a raw surprise alarm.
## What predictive surprise detects
Predictive surprise detects **poor one-step predictive fit**.
It can react to:
- outliers;
- sudden volatility shocks;
- observations that do not fit any likely regime;
- distributional mismatch;
- model misspecification;
- new behavior not well represented in the training data.
This is different from the previous detectors.
The hard switch detector detects:
$$\arg\max_j \alpha_{t|t}(j) \ne \arg\max_j \alpha_{t-1|t-1}(j)$$
The posterior transition detector detects movement in:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t}$$
The predictive surprise detector detects low:
$$p(y_t\mid y_{1:t-1},\hat{\theta})$$
So its semantic meaning is:
> The model did not expect the observation.

Not necessarily:
> The latent regime changed.
## Surprise does not necessarily mean regime switch
This distinction is important.
A surprising observation may occur without changing the dominant regime.
For exmpe, suppose the model is already in a high-volatility regime and remains there:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1} = (0.05,0.95)$$
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t} = (0.04,0.96)$$
The posterior barely changes.
The hard switch detector does not fire.
The posterior transition detector may also produce a small score.
But if the actual observation $y_t$ is extreme even for the high-volatility regime, then:
$$c_t$$
may still be very small, so:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{surp}} = -\log c_t$$
may be large.
### Interpretation
This is not a regime-switch signal.
It is a predictive-failure signal.
The model says:
> I still think this is the same regime, but the observation was unusually unlikely.

This is useful in financial data, where extreme returns or shocks may occur inside an already volatile regime.

## Surprise can fire before a posterior switch
The predictive surprise detector may also fire before the regime posterior clearly changes.
Suppose the model strongly predicts a calm regime:
$$\alpha_{t|t-1}(1)=0.95$$
$$\alpha_{t|t-1}(2)=0.05$$
Now a volatile observation arrives.
If the observation is much more plausible under regime 2 than regime 1, then the filter may start moving probability toward regime 2.
But even before the posterior fully switches, the predictive density:
$$c_t = f_1(y_t)\alpha_{t|t-1}(1) + f_2(y_t)\alpha_{t|t-1}(2)$$
may be small, because the volatile regime had low predicted probability.
So surprise can detect the moment when the observation contradicts the model’s previous prediction.
## Label invariance
The predictive surprise score is label-invariant.
The score is:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{surp}} = -\log \left( \sum_{j=1}^K f_j(y_t)\alpha_{t|t-1}(j) \right)$$
If regime labels are permuted, the terms in the sum are permuted, but the sum itself is unchanged.
Therefore:
$$c_t$$
does not depend on whether a regime is called “regime 1” or “regime 2”.
### Intuition
Hard switch and leave-previous detectors depend more directly on regime labels.
Predictive surprise only asks:
> How well did the entire mixture predictive distribution explain the observation?

So it is invariant to relabeling of regimes.
This is a useful property because regime labels in latent-variable models are arbitrary.
## Raw surprise is unbounded
Unlike the posterior transition scores, which lie in:
$$[0,1]$$
the raw surprise score is:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{surp}} = -\log c_t$$
This score is bounded below only depending on density scale and can be large when:
$$c_t$$
is very sall.
For continuous densities, $c_t$ can also be greater than $1$, depending on the scale of the data, so:
$$-\log c_t$$
can be negative.
That is not an error.
Densities are not probabilities; they can exceed $1$.
### Practical implication
Thresholds for surprise are not universal.
A threshold such as:
$$\tau=5$$
does not have the same meaning across all data transformations, sampling frequencies, or feature scales.
So surprise thresholds should be calibrated empirically, for example on a validation set or by inspecting the training distribution of surprise scores.
Do not expand threshold calibration here in detail. Threshold policy belongs in Section 4.7.
## Why use log predictive density
Using:
$$-\log c_t$$
rather than:
$$\frac{1}{c_t}​$$
or another inverse-density score has several advantages.
### Additivity
Log predictive densities add over time:
$$\log p(y_{1:T}\mid\hat{\theta}) = \sum_{t=1}^T \log c_t$$
So surprise is directly connected to the likelihood decomposition.
### Numerical stability
The filter already computes:
$$\log c_t$$
using log-space calculations.
So:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{surp}} = -\log c_t$$
is naturally available.
### Interpretability
A difference in surprise corresponds to a likelihood ratio.
If two observations have surprise scores:
$$s_a=-\log c_a$$
and:
$$s_b=-\log c_b$$
then:
$$s_a-s_b = -\log c_a + \log c_b = \log\frac{c_b}{c_a}$$
So if:
$$s_a-s_b=2$$
then observation $a$ had predictive density:
$$e^{-2}$$
times that of observation $b$.
This makes the score meaningful on the log scale.
## EMA-adjusted surprise
Raw surprise is the **default** score for this detector: $s_t = -\log c_t$ with no baseline adjustment. EMA adjustment is **opt-in** and is enabled by setting $\alpha_{\mathrm{EMA}}\in(0,1]$ in the detector configuration. When $\alpha_{\mathrm{EMA}}$ is unset, the detector uses the raw score and produces a result on the very first observation; when $\alpha_{\mathrm{EMA}}$ is set, the first step is used to initialise the baseline and is marked not-ready.

Raw surprise may have a changing baseline.
For example, during a turbulent market period, many observations may have higher surprise than during calm periods.
To detect observations that are surprising relative to recent surprise levels, an adaptive baseline can be used.
Let:
$$b_t$$
be an exponential moving average of raw surprise.
The baseline update is:
$$b_t = \alpha_{\mathrm{EMA}}s_t^{\mathrm{surp}} + (1-\alpha_{\mathrm{EMA}})b_{t-1}$$
where:
$$0<\alpha_{\mathrm{EMA}}\le 1$$
The adjusted surprise score is:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{adj}} = s_t^{\mathrm{surp}} - b_{t-1}$$
### Important causality detail
The score uses:
$$b_{t-1}$$
not:
$$b_t$$
This matters because $b_t$ already includes the current surprise:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{surp}}$$
If the score used $b_t$, the current observation would partially update its own baseline before being judged.
That would weaken the score and blur the causal interpretation.
So the causal order is:

```
1. observe y_t2. compute c_t3. compute raw surprise s_t^surp = -log c_t4. compute adjusted score s_t^adj = s_t^surp - b_{t-1}5. update baseline b_t
```
## Intuition behind EMA-adjusted surprise
The raw score asks:
> Was this observation unlikely under the fitted model?

The adjusted score asks:
> Was this observation more surprising than recent observations?

If the baseline is:
$$b_{t-1}=3$$
and the current raw surprise is:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{surp}}=8$$
then:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{adj}}=8-3=5.$$
This is a large positive adjusted surprise.
If:
$$b_{t-1}=7$$
and:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{surp}}=8$$
then:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{adj}}=8-7=1$$
The same raw surprise is less unusual relative to the recent baseline.
### Interpretation
EMA-adjusted surprise is useful when the absolute surprise level changes over time.
But it also changes the detector semantics:

| Score                 | Meaning                                       |
| --------------------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| raw surprise          | poor fit under fitted model                   |
| EMA-adjusted surprise | poor fit relative to recent surprise baseline |
## EMA parameter interpretation
The EMA update is:
$$b_t = \alpha_{\mathrm{EMA}}s_t^{\mathrm{surp}} + (1-\alpha_{\mathrm{EMA}})b_{t-1}$$
The parameter:
$$\alpha_{\mathrm{EMA}}$$
controls adaptation speed.
### Large $\alpha_{\mathrm{EMA}}$
If:
$$\alpha_{\mathrm{EMA}}$$
is large, the baseline reacts quickly to recent surprise.
This makes the adjusted detector more adaptive but may also absorb changes quickly.
### Small $\alpha_{\mathrm{EMA}}$
If:
$$\alpha_{\mathrm{EMA}}$$
is small, the baseline changes slowly.
This makes the adjusted detector more stable but less responsive to changes in the background surprise level.
### Intuition
The EMA baseline is a memory mechanism.
It summarizes recent surprise levels without storing the full history.
## First-step warmup
The EMA-adjusted detector needs an existing baseline:
$$b_{t-1}$$
At the first step, there is no previous baseline.
A simple initialization is:
$$b_1 = s_1^{\mathrm{surp}}$$
and return:
$$\text{ready} = \text{false}$$
for the first adjusted score.
From the second step onward:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{adj}} = s_t^{\mathrm{surp}} - b_{t-1}$$
is available.
### Intuition
The first surprise value initializes the baseline.
It should not usually be treated as a calibrated deviation from baseline, because no baseline existed yet.
## Raw versus adjusted surprise
The two variants answer different questions.

| Variant           | Formula                                          | Meaning                                                 |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------- |
| raw surprise      | $s_t^{\mathrm{surp}}=-\log c_t$                  | how unlikely $y_t$ was under the fitted model           |
| adjusted surprise | $s_t^{\mathrm{adj}}=s_t^{\mathrm{surp}}-b_{t-1}$ | how much more surprising $y_t$ was than recent baseline |

### Raw surprise is better when
- you want direct negative log predictive density;
- the fitted model is a fixed reference distribution;
- absolute predictive failures are important;
- thresholds are calibrated on validation data.
### Adjusted surprise is better when
- the background surprise level drifts;
- you care about spikes relative to recent behavior;
- you want to suppress periods where all observations have elevated surprise.
### Caution
Adjusted surprise may adapt to persistent distributional change.
That means after a new regime of high surprise persists long enough, the baseline may rise and alarms may stop.
This is not necessarily bad, but it should be understood.
## Thresholding surprise
The detector produces a score:
$$s_t$$
which can be either:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{surp}}$$
or:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{adj}}$$
A basic alarm rule is:
$$a_t = \mathbf{1}[s_t\ge \tau]$$
The threshold:
$$\tau$$
depends on the chosen score type.
For raw surprise, $\tau$ is on the negative-log-density scale.
For adjusted surprise, $\tau$ is on the excess-surprise scale.
The score may also pass through persistence and cooldown logic.

## Strengths of predictive surprise detector
### Detects predictive failure
It directly detects when the model assigns low probability to the new observation.
This is useful for abrupt shocks and outliers.
### Does not require a posterior regime switch
It can fire even if the filtered posterior remains in the same regime.
### Label-invariant
Because:
$$c_t = \sum_j f_j(y_t)\alpha_{t|t-1}(j)$$
the score is unchanged by relabeling regimes.
### Connected to likelihood
The score is exactly the negative one-step log-likelihood contribution:
$$-\log c_t$$
This gives a clear probabilistic interpretation.
## Limitations of predictive surprise detector
### Surprise is not the same as changepoint
A single outlier can generate high surprise without a persistent structural change.
So a surprise alarm should be interpreted as predictive anomaly, not automatically as a regime transition.
### Sensitive to scaling and feature choice
Since $c_t$ is a continuous density, the numerical scale of surprise depends on the scale of $y_t$.
Transforming the input series changes density values.
Therefore, surprise thresholds require empirical calibration.
### Can reflect model misspecification
High surprise may mean:
- actual changepoint;
- outlier;
- volatility shock;
- poor fit;
- wrong number of regimes;
- inappropriate Gaussian assumptions.
So surprise alarms require careful interpretation.
### EMA adjustment can hide persistent change
If the baseline adapts upward, persistent high surprise may stop triggering alarms.
This is useful for reducing repeated alarms but may reduce sensitivity to long changes.

## Key conceptual summary
The predictive density is:
$$c_t = p(y_t\mid y_{1:t-1},\hat{\theta}) = \sum_{j=1}^K f_j(y_t)\alpha_{t|t-1}(j)$$
The raw surprise score is:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{surp}} = -\log c_t$$

# 4.7 Alarm policy: thresholds, persistence, cooldown
## What this section should do
Sections 4.4–4.6 defined detector scores:
- hard switch score;
- posterior transition score;
- predictive surprise score.
Each detector produces a scalar score:
$$s_t$$
But a score is not yet an alarm.
This section explains how scores become final alarm events.
The basic problem is:
> Detector scores may fluctuate, spike, or oscillate. A practical online detector should not necessarily raise an alarm for every isolated threshold crossing.

Therefore, the alarm policy adds a decision layer:
$$s_t \longrightarrow a_t \in \{0,1\}$$
The policy is built from three components:
1. **threshold** — decide whether the score is large enough;
2. **persistence** — require enough consecutive threshold crossings;
3. **cooldown** — suppress repeated alarms after one has fired.
The purpose of this section is to define these rules precisely.
## What this section should not include
Do **not** redefine hard switch, posterior transition, or surprise detectors.
Only discuss how their scores are thresholded.
Do **not** discuss how to choose optimal thresholds experimentally.
That belongs in the evaluation or calibration chapter.
Do **not** discuss precision, recall, detection delay, or benchmark matching.
That belongs in the experimental chapter.
Do **not** discuss smoothing, EM, or offline parameter estimation.
This section is only about converting online scores into alarms.
The purpose is:
```
score → threshold crossing → persistence check → cooldown check → alarm event
```
## Detector score versus alarm
A detector score is a numerical signal:
$$s_t \in \mathbb{R}$$
Its meaning depends on the detector.

| Detector             | Score meaning                                     |
| -------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| hard switch          | dominant regime changed or not                    |
| posterior transition | filtered posterior moved                          |
| predictive surprise  | observation was unlikely under predictive density |

An alarm is a binary decision:
$$a_t \in \{0,1\}$$
where:
$$a_t=1$$
means an alarm is issued at time $t$, and:
$$a_t=0$$
means no alarm is issued.
The alarm policy therefore defines a function:
$$a_t = \mathcal{A}(s_1,\dots,s_t)$$
It may depend on current and previous scores, but not future scores.
This keeps the alarm policy causal.
## Basic threshold rule
The simplest alarm rule is a threshold crossing:
$$a_t^{\mathrm{raw}} = \mathbf{1}[s_t \ge \tau]$$
where:
- $s_t$ is the detector score;
- $\tau$ is the threshold;
- $\mathbf{1}[\cdot]$ is the indicator function.
So:
$$a_t^{\mathrm{raw}}= \begin{cases} 1, & s_t \ge \tau,\\ 0, & s_t < \tau. \end{cases}$$
### Intuition
The threshold says:
> Only treat the score as meaningful if it is large enough.

For example, if the score is predictive surprise:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{surp}}=-\log c_t$$
then a large score means the observation was unlikely under the fitted model.
If:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{surp}} \ge \tau$$
then the observation is surprising enough to be considered an alarm candidate.
## Different detectors have different score scales
The threshold must be interpreted relative to the detector score.

| Detector              | Score                           | Typical range               |
| --------------------- | ------------------------------- | --------------------------- |
| hard switch           | $s_t^{\mathrm{HS}}$             | ${0,1}$                     |
| leave-previous        | $s_t^{\mathrm{LP}}$             | $[0,1]$                     |
| total variation       | $s_t^{\mathrm{TV}}$             | $[0,1]$                     |
| raw surprise          | $s_t^{\mathrm{surp}}=-\log c_t$ | unbounded / scale-dependent |
| EMA-adjusted surprise | $s_t^{\mathrm{adj}}$            | can be negative or positive |

This matters because a threshold such as:
$$\tau=0.5$$
has a clear meaning for scores in $[0,1]$, but not for raw surprise.
For posterior transition scores:
$$\tau=0.5$$
means a large posterior movement.
For raw surprise:
$$\tau=0.5$$
may be weak or strong depending on the scale of the data and the fitted density.
### Practical implication
Each detector needs its own threshold.
Do not compare thresholds across detectors unless the score scales are comparable.
## Why thresholding alone may be too noisy
The raw threshold rule is:
$$a_t^{\mathrm{raw}} = \mathbf{1}[s_t\ge\tau]$$
This fires on every single threshold crossing.
That can be too sensitive.
### Example: isolated spike
Suppose a surprise score sequence is:
$$s_{1:7} = (1.2,1.4,1.3,7.8,1.5,1.3,1.4)$$
and:
$$\tau=5$$
Then only one score crosses the threshold:
$$s_4=7.8$$
A raw threshold rule would issue an alarm at $t=4$.
This may be appropriate if single outliers are important.
But if the goal is to detect persistent changepoints rather than isolated anomalies, this may be too sensitive.
### Example: repeated alarms during one event
Suppose:
$$s_{1:7} = (1.2,5.5,6.1,6.4,5.9,1.8,1.3)$$
$$\tau=5$$
The raw threshold rule fires at:
$$t=2,3,4,5$$
But these may all correspond to the same underlying event.
A cooldown rule prevents repeated alarms during the same episode.
## Persistence rule
The persistence rule requires a threshold crossing to last for several consecutive time steps.
Define the raw crossing indicator:
$$b_t = \mathbf{1}[s_t\ge\tau]$$
Let:
$$N_{\mathrm{req}}$$
be the required number of consecutive crossings.
The persistence condition is:
$$b_{t-N_{\mathrm{req}}+1} = b_{t-N_{\mathrm{req}}+2} = \cdots = b_t = 1$$
Equivalently:
$$\sum_{r=0}^{N_{\mathrm{req}}-1} b_{t-r} = N_{\mathrm{req}}$$
Only then is an alarm allowed.
So, before cooldown is considered:
$$a_t^{\mathrm{pers}} = \mathbf{1} \left[ \sum_{r=0}^{N_{\mathrm{req}}-1} b_{t-r} = N_{\mathrm{req}} \right]$$
### Intuition
Persistence asks:
> Is the detector score high consistently, or was it just a one-step spike?

If:
$$N_{\mathrm{req}}=1$$
then persistence is disabled and every threshold crossing is eligible.
If:
$$N_{\mathrm{req}}=3$$
then the score must cross the threshold for three consecutive time steps.
## Persistence example
Suppose:
$$\tau=5$$
and:
$$N_{\mathrm{req}}=3$$
Scores:
$$s_{1:8} = (1.2,5.4,5.8,6.1,1.7,5.5,5.9,1.6)$$
Raw crossings:
$$b_{1:8} = (0,1,1,1,0,1,1,0)$$
The first three consecutive crossings occur at:
$$t=2,3,4$$
So the persistence condition is satisfied at:
$$t=4$$
No alarm fires at $t=2$ or $t=3$ because the detector has not yet observed enough consecutive crossings.
The later crossings at $t=6,7$ do not satisfy persistence because there are only two consecutive crossings.
### Interpretation
Persistence delays alarms but reduces isolated false alarms.
The delay introduced by persistence is approximately:
$$N_{\mathrm{req}}-1$$
time steps after the first threshold crossing.
## Recursive implementation of persistence
Instead of storing the full recent history, the policy can store a counter.
Let:
$$m_t$$
be the number of consecutive threshold crossings ending at time $t$.
Update:
$$m_t = \begin{cases} m_{t-1}+1, & s_t \ge \tau,\\ 0, & s_t < \tau. \end{cases}$$
The persistence condition is:
$$m_t \ge N_{\mathrm{req}}$$
So:
$$a_t^{\mathrm{pers}} = \mathbf{1}[m_t \ge N_{\mathrm{req}}]$$
### Intuition
The counter remembers how long the score has remained above the threshold.
If the score drops below threshold, the counter resets to zero.
This is exactly the informaton needed for persistence.
## Cooldown rul
Persistence controls when an alarm is allowed to fire.
Cooldown controls how soon another alarm may fire after one has already fired.
Let:
$$C$$
be the cooldown length.
After an alarm fires at time $t^\star$, new alarms are suppressed for the next $C$ steps.
That means alarms are not allowed at:
$$t^\star+1,\dots,t^\star+C$$
A new alarm can only be issued after the cooldown expires.
### Intuition
Cooldown prevents repeated alarms for the same transition episode.
Without cooldown, if a score remains above threshold for many steps, the detector may fire repeatedly.
Cooldown says:
> Once an alarm is issued, assume the current event has already been reported. Wait before issuing another one.
## Cooldown example
Suppose:
$$\tau=5$$
$$N_{\mathrm{req}}=1$$
and:
$$C=3$$
Scores:
$$s_{1:8} = (1.0,5.5,6.0,5.7,5.8,1.2,6.2,6.4)$$
Without cooldown, alarms would fire at:
$$t=2,3,4,5,7,8$$
With cooldown:
- alarm fires at $t=2$;
- cooldown suppresses $t=3,4,5$;
- cooldown expires after $t=5$;
- alarm can fire again at $t=7$;
- then cooldown suppresses $t=8$.
So final alarm times are:
$$t=2,\quad t=7$$
### Interpretation
The cooldown compresses clusters of threshold crossings into fewer alarm events.
## Combined policy
The full alarm policy combines threshold, persistence, and cooldown.
First compute the threshold crossing:
$$b_t = \mathbf{1}[s_t\ge\tau]$$
Update the consecutive crossing count:
$$m_t = \begin{cases} m_{t-1}+1, & b_t=1,\\ 0, & b_t=0. \end{cases}$$
Let:
$$d_t$$
be the cooldown counter before issuing an alarm at time $t$.
If:
$$d_t>0$$
then the policy is in cooldown and no alarm may be issued:
$$a_t=0$$
If:
$$d_t=0$$
and:
$$m_t \ge N_{\mathrm{req}}$$
then an alarm is issued:
$$a_t=1$$
After issuing an alarm, set cooldown to:
$$C$$
Otherwise:
$$a_t=0$$
### Compact decision rule
Ignoring update-order details, the alarm condition is:
$$a_t = \mathbf{1} [ s_t \ge \tau ] \cdot \mathbf{1} [ m_t \ge N_{\mathrm{req}} ] \cdot \mathbf{1} [ d_t=0 ]$$
More precisely, since $m_t$ already includes the threshold crossing information:
$$a_t = \mathbf{1} [ m_t \ge N_{\mathrm{req}} ] \cdot \mathbf{1} [ d_t=0 ]$$
## Update order
A clean causal update order is:
```
1. Receive score s_t.2. Compute threshold crossing:       b_t = 1[s_t >= τ]3. Update consecutive crossing count:       if b_t = 1:           m_t = m_{t-1} + 1       else:           m_t = 04. Check cooldown:       if cooldown_remaining > 0:           no alarm           decrease cooldown counter       else:           if m_t >= N_req:               issue alarm               set cooldown_remaining = C           else:               no alarm
```
This policy uses only current and past information.
Therefore, it is causal.
## Interaction between persistence and cooldown
Persistence and cooldown solve different problems.

| Mechanism   | Purpose                                          |
| ----------- | ------------------------------------------------ |
| threshold   | defines what counts as a high score              |
| persistence | suppresses isolated spikes                       |
| cooldown    | suppresses repeated alarms from the same episode |

### Persistence increases detection delay
If:
$$N_{\mathrm{req}}>1$$
then the detector must wait for several consecutive crossings.
This can reduce false alarms but delays detection.
### Cooldown reduces repeated alarms
If:
$$C>0$$
then the detector cannot fire again immediately after an alarm.
This can reduce alarm spam but may miss closely spaced changes.
### Practical trade-off
Increasing persistence and cooldown makes the detector more conservative.
Decreasing them makes the detector more sensitive.
## Detector-specific threshold interpretation
The same alarm policy can be shared across detectors, but thresholds mean different things.
### Hard switch
For hard switch:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{HS}}\in\{0,1\}$$
A threshold such as:
$$\tau=0.5$$
means:
> alarm candidate whenever the hard switch score is 1.

Persistence then means:
> require multiple consecutive hard switch signals.

This is strict and may rarely happen unless the detector can produce consecutive switches.
So for hard switch, persistence should be used carefully.
### Posterior transition
For posterior transition:
$$s_t\in[0,1]$$
A threshold such as:
$$\tau=0.3$$
means:
> alarm candidate when posterior movement is at least 0.3.

Persistence means posterior movement must remain large across multiple steps.
### Predictive surprise
For raw surprise:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{surp}}=-\log c_t$$
The threshold is on the negative log predictive density scale.
A larger threshold means the detector only reacts to stronger predictive failures.
For EMA-adjusted surprise:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{adj}}=s_t^{\mathrm{surp}}-b_{t-1}$$
The threshold means:
> current surprise exceeds recent baseline by at least $\tau$.
## Alarm event interpretation
When an alarm fires, the event can be represented as:
$$(t, s_t, D)$$
where:
- $t$ is the alarm time;
- $s_t$ is the score that triggered it;
- $D$ is the detector type or detector metadata.
In the implementation, each alarm event also records the dominant regime *before* and *after* the alarm:
- the **after** field $\arg\max_j\alpha_{t|t}(j)$ is always populated;
- the **before** field $\arg\max_j\alpha_{t-1|t-1}(j)$ is populated for the hard-switch and posterior-transition detectors and is intentionally left empty for the predictive-surprise detector, since a surprise alarm has no semantic notion of a "previous dominant regime" (it is a predictive-failure event, not a regime-transition event).
Optionally, one may further store:
- threshold $\tau$;
- score type;
- posterior vector;
- predictive density;
- cooldown state.
The threshold $\tau$ in particular is not part of the event itself in this implementation; it lives in the detector configuration snapshot persisted alongside the run, which is consistent with calling it optional event metadata.
This is useful for later evaluation and visualization.
### Intuition
The alarm is not just a boolean.
For analysis, you want to know:
> Why did the detector fire?

The score and detector type provide the explanation.
## Causality of the alarm policy
The alarm policy is causal because it uses only:
$$s_1,\dots,s_t$$
Thresholding uses $s_t$.
Persistence uses recent past scores:
$$s_{t-N_{\mathrm{req}}+1},\dots,s_t$$
Cooldown uses time since previous alarms.
None of these require future scores:
$$s_{t+1},s_{t+2},\dots$$
Therefore, if the score is causal, the alarm policy remains causal.
This is important for online changepoint detection.

# 4.8 Discussion of detector semantics
## What this section should do
This section should explain that the three detectors are **not equivalent**, even if they all output the same type of final object:
$$a_t \in \{0,1\}$$
An alarm from one detector does not mean exactly the same thing as an alarm from another detector.
The central point is:
$$\text{alarm} \neq \text{true changepoint}$$
More precisely:
> An alarm means that a detector-specific score exceeded its alarm policy. The meaning of that alarm depends on how the score was defined.

So Section 4.8 should compare the semantics of:
1. hard switch detector;
2. posterior transition detector;
3. predictive surprise detector.
This section should **not** introduce new algorithms. It should clarify how to interpret the algorithms already defined in Sections 4.4–4.7.
## What this section should not include
Do **not** rederive the formulas of each detector in full. They were already defined in Sections 4.4–4.6.
Do **not** explain the alarm-olicy mechanics again. Section 4.7 already covered thresholds, persistence, and cooldown.
Do **not** discuss evaluation metrics such as precision, recall, delay, or F1.
Do **not** introduce new detectors.
Do **not** introduce model retraining, threshold calibration, or validation methodology.

## Shared structure of all detectors
All detectors follow the same abstract form:
$$s_t = g(I_t)$$
where $I_t$ is the causal online input produced by the filter.
Then the alarm policy maps:
$$s_t \longrightarrow a_t$$
A basic threshold rule would be:
$$a_t = \mathbf{1}[s_t \ge \tau]$$
With persistence and cooldown, the rule is more conservative, but the interpretation remains:
> The detector fired because its own score became large enough according to its policy.

The important part is that the score $s_t$ has a different meaning for each detector.
## Hard switch semantics
The hard switch detector uses the dominant filtered regime:
$$\hat{S}_t = \arg\max_j \alpha_{t|t}(j)$$
It detects whether this dominant label changed:
$$\hat{S}_t \ne \hat{S}_{t-1}$$
With a confidence gate, the detector also requires:
$$\max_j \alpha_{t|t}(j) \ge \tau_{\mathrm{conf}}$$
So its score can be summarized as:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{HS}} = \mathbf{1}[\hat{S}_t \ne \hat{S}_{t-1}] \cdot \mathbf{1} \left[ \max_j \alpha_{t|t}(j) \ge \tau_{\mathrm{conf}} \right]$$
### Semantic meaning
A hard switch alarm means:
> The most probable filtered regime changed, with sufficient posterior confidence.

It is a **dominant-label event**.
It does not mean that the full posterior changed dramatically. It also does not mean that the observation was predictively surprising.
### Best interpretation
This detector is best interpreted as evidence of a **sharp latent-regime transition** under the fitted Markov-switching model.
It is especially meaningful when the posterior changes from something like:
$$(0.95,0.05) \quad \text{to} \quad (0.10,0.90)$$
In that case, the model clearly moved from one dominant regime to another.
### Weakness
The hard switch detector discards posterior uncertainty.
For example:
$$(0.51,0.49) \quad \text{to} \quad (0.49,0.51)$$
changes the argmax, but the model is uncertain at both time points.
Also:
$$(0.95,0.05) \quad \text{to} \quad (0.60,0.40)$$
may be a meaningful posterior movement, but the dominant regime does not change.
So hard switch alarms are easy to interpret, but they are coarse.
## Posterior transition semantics
The posterior transition detector does not collapse the posterior into one label. It uses movement in the filtered posterior distribution:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1} \to \boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t}$$
There are two variants.
### Leave-previous score
Define the previously dominant regime:
$$r_{t-1} = \arg\max_j \alpha_{t-1|t-1}(j)$$
Then:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{LP}} = 1-\alpha_{t|t}(r_{t-1})$$
This score measures how much posterior mass has left the previously dominant regime.
### Total variation score
The total variation score is:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{TV}} = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{j=1}^K \left| \alpha_{t|t}(j) - \alpha_{t-1|t-1}(j) \right|$$
This measures how much the whole posterior distribution changed.
### Semantic meaning
A posterior transition alarm means:
> The filtered posterior distribution over regimes changed substantially.

It is a **posterior-mass movement event**.
It does not necessarily mean that the dominant regime changed. It also does not necessarily mean that the observation was unlikely under the predictive distribution.
### Best interpretation
This detector is useful when changes are gradual.
For example:
$$(0.95,0.05) \to (0.80,0.20) \to (0.60,0.40)$$
The dominant regime may remain the same, but the model is steadily losing confidence in it.
The posterior transition detector can capture this earlier than the hard switch detector.
### Weakness
Posterior movement is not identical to predictive failure.
A posterior vector can move because the new observation shifted regime probabilities, even if the observation was not extremely unlikely overall.
Also, the total variation score says **how much** mass moved, but not necessarily **why** it moved or whether the movement corresponds to an externally meaningful changepoint.
So this detector should be interpreted as a soft regime-belief shift, not as direct proof of a structural break.
## Predictive surprise semantics
The predictive surprise detector uses the one-step-ahead predictive density:
$$c_t = p(y_t\mid y_{1:t-1},\hat{\theta})$$
The raw surprise score is:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{surp}} = -\log c_t$$
A large value means that $c_t$ is small, so the observation was unlikely under the fitted model before observing it.
### Semantic meaning
A predictive surprise alarm means:
> The new observation had low one-step-ahead predictive density under the offline-trained Markov-switching model.

It is a **predictive-failure event**.
It does not necessarily mean that the latent regime changed.
### Best interpretation
This detector is useful for:
- outliers;
- volatility shocks;
- sudden predictive failures;
- observations outside the learned regime structure;
- distributional mismatch.
For example, if the posterior remains:
$$(0.05,0.95) \to (0.04,0.96)$$
then neither hard switch nor posterior transition may fire.
But if the observation is extreme even under the high-volatility regime, then:
$$c_t$$
may be very small, producing a large:
$$-\log c_t$$
So surprise can detect anomalies inside an already dominant regime.
### Weakness
A high surprise value can mean several things:
- a genuine changepoint;
- an isolated outlier;
- a volatility shock;
- model misspecification;
- heavy-tailed behavior not captured by Gaussian emissions;
- poor fit of the offline-trained model to current data.
Therefore, a surprise alarm should be interpreted as:
> The fitted model did not predict this observation well.

Not as:
> The regime definitely changed.
## Semantic comparison table
A useful table for this section:

| Detector            | Score                                   | Alarm means                            | Best for                                     | Main limitation                            |
| ------------------- | --------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| Hard switch         | $\mathbf{1}[\hat{S}_t\ne\hat{S}_{t-1}]$ | dominant filtered regime changed       | sharp regime transitions                     | ignores most posterior uncertainty         |
| Leave-previous      | $1-\alpha_{t                            | t}(r_{t-1})$                           | posterior mass left previous dominant regime | early loss of confidence in current regime |
| Total variation     | $\frac12\sum_j                          | \alpha_{t                              | t}(j)-\alpha_{t-1                            | t-1}(j)                                    |
| Predictive surprise | $-\log c_t$                             | observation had low predictive density | shocks, outliers, anomalies                  | surprise does not imply regime transition  |

This table is probably the most important part of the section.
## Sensitivity ordering
You can include a short conceptual ordering.
From most discrete to most continuous:
$$\text{hard switch} \quad \to \quad \text{posterior transition} \quad \to \quad \text{predictive surprise}$$
But this is not a strict dominance ranking. It is a semantic distinction.
### Hard switch
Least sensitive to gradual movement.
It only reacts when:
$$\arg\max_j \alpha_{t|t}(j)$$
changes.
### Posterior transition
More sensitive to gradual regime-belief movement.
It can react before the argmax changes.
### Predictive surprise
Sensitive to predictive anomalies even when posterior regime probabilities remain stable.
It can fire without any meaningful posterior transition.
So the detectors answer different questions:

| Question                                   | Detector             |
| ------------------------------------------ | -------------------- |
| Did the most likely regime label change?   | hard switch          |
| Did posterior regime belief move?          | posterior transition |
| Was the observation unexpectedly unlikely? | predictive surprise  |
## Why alarms are not ground-truth changepoints
This section should explicitly warn against overinterpretation.
The detector output is:
$$a_t \in \{0,1\}$$
But:
$$a_t=1$$
does not prove that a true changepoint occurred.
It only means:
$$\text{chosen score} + \text{chosen alarm policy} \Rightarrow \text{alarm}$$
A more precise statement is:
> An alarm is an operational decision produced by a detector, not an observed fact about the data-generating process.

This matters because in financial data, true changepoints are rarely directly labeled. A detected event is usually an inference signal, not ground truth.
## Causality of detector semantics
All three detectors are causal because they use only quantities available at time $t$.
They depend on:
$$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t|t}, \quad \boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t-1|t-1}, \quad c_t$$
all of which are computed from:
$$y_{1:t}$$
They do not use smoothed probabilities:
$$\gamma_t(j)=\Pr(S_t=j\mid y_{1:T})$$
because smoothing would use future observations and would violate the online setting.
So the semantic interpretation is always:
> what the detector could know at time $t$,

not:
> what the model can infer after seeing the whole sample.

This distinction is important when comparing online alarms to retrospective regime plots.
## Frozen-parameter assumption
The detectors also depend on the frozen fitted model:
\hat{\theta}
That means all scores are interpreted relative to the model trained offline.
The online filter asks:
> Under the fitted parameters $\hat{\theta}$, what is the current posterior regime belief and predictive density?

This is important because if the market changes in a way that is not represented in the training data, then the detector may react in different ways.
### Possible cases
#### Case 1: new behavior resembles a known regime
The posterior may shift toward that regime.
Hard switch or posterior transition may fire.
#### Case 2: new behavior is unlike all learned regimes
The predictive density may become low:
$$c_t \downarrow$$
so predictive surprise may fire.
#### Case 3: model adapts posterior but still predicts poorly
The posterior may remain stable or shift only slightly, while surprise remains high.
This suggests mismatch between the incoming data and the fitted model.
### Thesis wording
A good sentence:
> The detectors should be interpreted relative to the frozen offline-trained model; an alarm indicates a change or anomaly with respect to the learned regime structure, not an absolute ground-truth changepoint.
## Detector choice depends on thesis goal
This section can briefly explain when each detector is suitable.
### Use hard switch when
You want a simple, interpretable detector for clear regime changes.
It is useful when the fitted regimes are well separated and posterior probabilities switch sharply.
### Use posterior transition when
You want sensitivity to soft or gradual shifts in regime belief.
It is useful when regime transitions are not instantaneous or posterior uncertainty rises before a switch.
### Use predictive surprise when
You want to detect observations that the model did not predict well.
It is useful for shocks, anomalies, and financial extremes.
But its alarms should be interpreted as predictive anomalies, not necessarily regime switches.

## Key conceptual summary
Hard switch detects:
$$\hat{S}_t \ne \hat{S}_{t-1}$$
Posterior transition detects posterior movement:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{TV}} = \frac{1}{2} \sum_j |\alpha_{t|t}(j)-\alpha_{t-1|t-1}(j)|$$
Predictive surprise detects predictive failure:
$$s_t^{\mathrm{surp}} = -\log c_t$$
The central distinction is:
$$\text{alarm} \neq \text{true changepoint}$$
In one sentence:
> Detector semantics specify what an alarm means: a hard switch alarm means a dominant regime-label flip, a posterior transition alarm means movement of regime posterior mass, and a predictive surprise alarm means low one-step predictive density under the frozen fitted model.
